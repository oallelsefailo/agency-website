Magento Reporting

Magento 2 Reporting Dashboard: What to Track

The KPIs that belong in every Magento reporting dashboard, and why the default reporting tools rarely give you the full picture in one place.

Why the default Magento reporting falls short

Magento 2 ships with a functional reporting suite: sales, products, customers, abandoned carts, and search terms. For a merchant checking yesterday's orders, it does the job. The limits show up when a team needs one weekly view across traffic, search, and revenue.

First, the reports are siloed. Magento reports know about orders and products. They do not show where traffic came from, which pages are gaining or losing search visibility, or whether a revenue dip lines up with a CTR decline on a key landing page. You need GA4 and Search Console for that context, and those are separate tools with separate tabs.

Second, they are passive. Every chart still needs someone to read it, explain it, and decide what to do next. There is no built-in summary that compares the data and calls out what changed.

Third, they do not scale cleanly across clients. An agency managing three Magento stores can end up with three reporting stacks, three spreadsheet workflows, and three separate weekly reviews.

The five KPIs every Magento reporting dashboard should surface

1. Traffic and conversion: sessions, engagement rate, purchase revenue

A reporting dashboard should open with the top-line GA4 metrics that tell you whether the store is healthy: sessions, engaged sessions, conversion rate, and purchase revenue. These four numbers, shown together with a prior-period comparison, answer the first question in any weekly review: are we up, down, or flat? Without this view, the review starts with guesswork.

2. Search visibility: impressions, CTR, average position

Search Console data belongs next to revenue data, not in a separate tab. When a product page loses revenue, the dashboard should show whether organic impressions dropped, whether average position shifted, or whether CTR declined while impressions held steady. Each pattern points to a different fix: a ranking issue, a snippet problem, or a seasonal demand shift. Without the paired data, diagnosis takes longer than it should.

3. Order-line revenue and product movement

Magento order data should be pulled at the line-item level, not flattened into vague totals. A dashboard that shows only total revenue hides the fact that one product dropped 40% while another doubled. Category-level revenue movement, average order value trends, top products by revenue, and product drop-off detection should all be visible in one view.

4. Priority review targets

A good reporting dashboard should do more than show charts. It should point out the pages, products, and categories worth reviewing. Low-CTR pages with impressions but no clicks. Products with revenue declines against the prior period. Categories that are outperforming or underperforming expectations. With the evidence attached, the weekly review becomes a decision-making session instead of a data-reading exercise.

5. An AI brief that reads across sources

An AI executive brief should read GA4, Search Console, and Magento order data together, then write a plain-English weekly summary. It should cite specific evidence: "Sessions are up 12% week over week, led by the Accessories category. The product page for SKU-4821 lost 34% of organic impressions. Check whether a competitor launched a replacement." This is not a generic template. It is a summary based on the connected sources.
